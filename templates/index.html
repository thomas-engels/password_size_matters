Together these characteristics determine the difficulty of cracking the password. More allowable characters, harder to crack. Longer password, exponentially hard to crack. Simply raise the number of allowable characters to the power of the password length to determine the number of possible passwords... you will need a calculator! Or you can use my calculator and table; which demonstrates why password length is far more impactful than special characters.

Secondly, you must understand what companies and websites do after you create your password. If they're a quality service, they likely transform your password using a complex, deterministic, "one-way" function that results in a 64-character list of seemingly random numbers and letters. This long random string is called the password hash, which is then stored on the company's servers. In fact, they should never know your plain-text password, only your password hash.

For the technically curious, I recommend this article explaining the nuances between hashing, encryption, and salting: https://www.thesslstore.com/blog/difference-encryption-hashing-salting/).

Let's assume your favorite company just suffered a major data breach (e.g., TicketMaster in June 2024) and the hackers stole millions of password hashes. The hackers have two choices now: sell the password hashes on the dark web or start doing lots of math. By "Math", I'm referring to hackers generating millions and millions of password hashes with the hope of finding a match from the data breach's hashes. If a match is found, then the hacker simply looks at what plain-text password generated the matching hash, password cracked! This is "Password Cracking": generating password hashes and discovering matches from the data breach.

After finding a match, the hacker's computer program will attempt to log in to hundreds of websites using with your username and password. If they successfully log in to a website, you've officially been hacked. And if you re-used that password, well you just made the hacker extra happy. Don't reuse any passwords.

In many cases, the hackers will prioritize generating hashes for the minimum password requirement types; e.g., TicketMaster requires an 8-character password containing at least 1 number and 1 letter. If you're a normal person, your passwords likely do not exceed these minimum requirements because it's convenient. Given the time and cost to generate hashes, hackers thus may only focus on these minimum requirements when generating their hashes. If TicketMaster is using one of the most secure password encryption algorithms (bcrypt), it will take roughly 12 days to generate EVERY password hash with less than $2M. If TickerMaster is using a less secure, yet common encryption algorithm (MD5), it will take less than 1 second to generate EVERY password hash for less than $2K.

As a TicketMaster customer, should I be concerned? This depends on the length of my password. If my password is 8 characters, yes I should be concerned and need to reset my password. If my password is long (15 to 20 characters), I could reset my password to be extra careful but cracking my password will take forever so the hackers won't even bother.

So what's the most important thing to remember from this website?

Long passwords (15-20) and NEVER re-using them will make you highly resilient to hackers. Cracking such a long password simply isn't worth the hacker's time and money. And even if they manage to crack it, their access will be limited to a single service.

Assumptions and Limitations:

  • My table was inspired by Hive Systems' Annual Table.
  • The "Green Passwords" are still crackable, but they will greatly minimize your risk exposure; even in the face of major GPU advancements.
  • Cracking times assume your password is randomly generated; not a human-written password. There are only 50,000 active English words so hackers prioritize them in search over random letters and numbers. (Another major benefit of Password Managers!)
  • The cost estimates ONLY account for purchasing NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs; they do not account for additional hardware and operating costs like electricity and Mountain Dew.
  • Password types and respective character counts:
    • Numbers Only (0-9) - 10
